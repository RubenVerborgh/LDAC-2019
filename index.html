Despite our strong focus on identity,
we struggle to find our own.

  • What unites a Semantic Web research community?
    • Is it Web?
    • Is it Semantics?
  • We’ve emerged at the intersection of other domains.
    • databases
    • logic
    • Web
    • …
  • What are the semantics we can attach
    to our own identity?

Exactly how much semantics
is enough semantics?

A little semantics
goes a long way.

Jim Hendler

Exactly how much semantics
is too much semantics?

Ontologies are like underwear.

Everyone agrees they’re a good idea,
but few people like to reuse someone else’s.

anonymous

Domain models are only as valuable as
the scope of the underlying consensus.

  • Ontology engineering methodologies
    often use clean room processes.
    • formalization by domain experts
  • What about parties that were not involved
    in the consensus process?
    • selective reuse (if any at all)
    • loose interpretation of semantics

Core frameworks such as RDF and OWL
are perceived as complex by developers.

  • Developers want to mark up data.
    • lack of deeper understanding
    • different from existing development practice
  • Ontologies are in practice often
    dumbed down to vocabularies.
    • stripping the data from semantics
    • once again leaving it up to individual applications

Schema.org fills the void for
a more pragmatic audience.

  • Such markup is handy for discovery and search,
    but limited for other purposes.
  • This leads to a heterogeneous
    and underspecified Web of Data.
  • Great for big harvesters,
    much less so for individual agents.

There is a mismatch between ontologies
in theory and in practice.

  • owl:sameAs performs great in experiments
    • …but it has so many different meanings
      that it has lost all meaning in practice.
  • Reasoning bridges gaps between ontologies.
    • In practice, you better write your query in exactly
      the same ontology as the SPARQL endpoint’s
    • …because in practice, reasoning is never switched on.

If semantics do no truly set us apart,
then maybe it is the Web.

  • The Web is vastly different from databases.
    • infinite domain of discourse
    • contradictions allowed and encouraged
  • No single RDF document contains the truth.
    • Any sufficiently large collection of documents
      will be internally inconsistent,
      and thus allow us to derive any truth.

“Web” is our main argument in
justifications for RDF’s complexity.

  • Our problems span the virtual address space
    of the globe and in fact the universe.
  • Our ontologies are spread all over the place.
    • Some of them even don’t dereference anymore!
  • The Web is the reason why
    we cannot all just use Schema.org.

So why are we so afraid then
to take our work to the Web?

  • It works in our university basement
    is an acceptable and applauded narrative.
  • We claim the Web as source of our intricacies,
    while ignoring all of the Web’s difficulties.
  • The Web looks nothing like your basement.

What is the external validity
of our controlled experiments?

  • Controlled experiments lead to valuable insights
    through easily reproducible findings.
  • If the discrepancy with actual conditions
    becomes too large, findings become irrelevant.
  • When a measure becomes a target,
    it ceases to be a good measure.
    • But was it ever?

Are we solving all the problems
we should be solving?

The Semantic Web research community
might have a habit of handwaving.

  • This paper presents the (hard) research,
    engineers will do the (simple) rest.
    • If the gap between theory and practice is so big,
      much more research is likely required.

The Web is a good platform for data publication,
but a pretty bad platform for data consumption.

Frank van Harmelen
