Despite our strong focus on identity,
we struggle to find our own.

  • What unites a Semantic Web research community?
    • Is it Web?
    • Is it Semantics?
  • We’ve emerged at the intersection of other domains.
    • databases
    • logic
    • Web
    • …
  • What are the semantics we can attach
    to our own identity?

Exactly how much semantics
is enough semantics?

A little semantics
goes a long way.

Jim Hendler

Exactly how much semantics
is too much semantics?

Ontologies are like underwear.

Everyone agrees they’re a good idea,
but few people like to reuse someone else’s.

anonymous

Domain models are only as valuable as
the scope of the underlying consensus.

  • Ontology engineering methodologies
    often use clean room processes.
    • formalization by domain experts
  • What about parties that were not involved
    in the consensus process?
    • selective reuse (if any at all)
    • loose interpretation of semantics

Core frameworks such as RDF and OWL
are perceived as complex by developers.

  • Developers want to mark up data.
    • lack of deeper understanding
    • different from existing development practice
  • Ontologies are in practice often
    dumbed down to vocabularies.
    • stripping the data from semantics
    • once again leaving it up to individual applications

Schema.org fills the void for
a more pragmatic audience.

  • Such markup is handy for discovery and search,
    but limited for other purposes.
  • This leads to a heterogeneous
    and underspecified Web of Data.
  • Great for big harvesters,
    much less so for individual agents.

There is a mismatch between ontologies
in theory and in practice.

  • owl:sameAs performs great in experiments
    • …but it has so many different meanings
      that it has lost all meaning in practice.
  • Reasoning bridges gaps between ontologies.
    • In practice, you better write your query in exactly
      the same ontology as the SPARQL endpoint’s
    • …because in practice, reasoning is never switched on.

If semantics do no truly set us apart,
then maybe it is the Web.

  • The Web is vastly different from databases.
    • infinite domain of discourse
    • contradictions allowed and encouraged
  • No single RDF document contains the truth.
    • Any sufficiently large collection of documents
      will be internally inconsistent,
      and thus allow us to derive any truth.

“Web” is our main argument in
justifications for RDF’s complexity.

  • Our problems span the virtual address space
    of the globe and in fact the universe.
  • Our ontologies are spread all over the place.
    • Some of them even don’t dereference anymore!
  • The Web is the reason why
    we cannot all just use Schema.org.

So why are we so afraid then
to take our work to the Web?

  • It works in our university basement
    is an acceptable and applauded narrative.
  • We claim the Web as source of our intricacies,
    while ignoring all of the Web’s difficulties.
  • The Web looks nothing like your basement.

What is the external validity
of our controlled experiments?

  • Controlled experiments lead to valuable insights
    through easily reproducible findings.
  • If the discrepancy with actual conditions
    becomes too large, findings become irrelevant.
  • When a measure becomes a target,
    it ceases to be a good measure.
    • But was it ever?

Are we solving all the problems
we should be solving?

The Semantic Web research community
might have a habit of handwaving.

  • This paper presents the (hard) research,
    engineers will do the (simple) rest.
    • If the gap between theory and practice is so big,
      much more research is likely required.

The Web is a good platform for data publication,
but a pretty bad platform for data consumption.

Frank van Harmelen

When looking for an identity
it helps to know who you are.

2012
We are totally doing Big Data.
2016
We are totally doing blockchain.
2019
We are totally doing AI.

And I agree on that last point (just not necessarily ML).

The Semantic Web teenage years
have perhaps been a bit confusing.

  • We’re almost being apologetic
    about not being something else.
  • Let’s not try to be the cool kid.
  • What are our core strengths?
    What challenges are we best positioned to tackle?

Perhaps Linked Data is actually
more challenging than Big Data.

  • An huge amount of homogeneous data in one place
    creates ideal conditions for processing and analysis.
  • Size does matter, just not always
    in the way others think.
    • A small number of very large data sets is easier to manage
      than a very large number of small data sets.
    • The level of heterogeneity and distribution of Linked Data
      cannot be tackled adequately with Big Data techniques.

Big Data is finite,
no matter how big it gets.

  • The Big Data fallacy disregards diminishing returns.
    • At some point, harvesting more data does not
      yield proportionally more knowledge anymore.
  • Very good reasons exist
    for data to live in different places.
    • legal
    • organizational
    • economical

The Web strives to be universal
through independence of many factors.

  • Anyone can use the Web, regardless of:
    hardware
    desktop
    phone
    tablet
    watch
    …
    software
    operating system
    browser
    app
    …
  • Developers are free to innovate.
    • build for the Web
    • standards provide interoperability

The Web brings freedom of expression
to everyone across the world.

  • Anyone can say anything about anything.
  • We all have our own spaces,
    so we don’t have to agree.
  • We can link to opinions of others
    to discuss about them.

People started their own blogs and sites,
sharing things on their own terms.

A generation of social platforms
helped people interact and share.

The Web brings permissionless innovation
at a global scale.

  • Anyone can build anything for any reason.
  • You don’t need anyone’s permission
    to join the Web and launch a new idea.
    • not the case in app stores

Permissionless innovation has brought
unprecedented creativity to the world.

Like other systems at the time,
the Web was designed decentrally.

  • Internet
  • email
  • NNTP
  • …

Its novelty was universality.

The first threat to universality
were the browser wars.

  • No Internet Explorer?
    • you’re out of luck!
  • People needed a certain computer.
    • no mobile devices
  • Developers depended on Microsoft’s pace.
    • browsers were not very powerful

This battle was replaced by another:
the search engine wars.

  • Not on Google?
    • you’re out of luck!
  • Ranking high became more important
    than high-quality content.
  • The reach of your innovations
    depended on one crawler.

This battle was also replaced by another:
the platform wars.

  • Not on Facebook?
    • you’re out of luck!
  • Clicks became more important
    than high-quality content.
  • You either compete for innovation
    with Facebook, or depend on it.

Our data has become centralized
in a handful of Web platforms.

  • People’s personal blogs
    are now on Facebook and Twitter.
    • great user experience
    • but we lost control
  • This has far-reaching consequences for privacy.
  • It endangers the Web’s universality.
    • Sign in with Facebook to see this content.
    • Facebook works better with the native app.

Within the walled gardens of social media,
you have to move either data or people.

The current massive centralization
hurts diversity, innovation, and choice.

  • If you can build 1 API integration
    • will it be facebook.com or private-identity-provider.org?
  • Developers depend on centralized platforms
    for data and identity.
    • …or they have to become such a platform themselves
  • People lose control of their data
    and cannot easily switch to other apps.
    • innovation cannot attract locked-in customers

Ironically, permissionless innovation
even allows platforms that prevent it.

The Facebook founder has no intention of
allowing anyone to build anything on his platform
that does not have his express approval.

Having profited mightily from the Web’s openness,
he has kicked away the ladder that elevated him
to his current eminence.

John Naughton, The Guardian
